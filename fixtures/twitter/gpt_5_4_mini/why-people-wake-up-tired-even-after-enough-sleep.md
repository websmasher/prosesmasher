1/10
Sleeping long enough is not the same as sleeping well. You can log 8 hours and still wake up wrecked if the sleep was broken, shallow, or full of stress.

2/10
The body cares about sleep quality, not just the clock on the wall. Waking often, snoring, or gasping can leave you tired even when bedtime looks “normal.”

3/10
Sleep apnea is a common reason. Breathing pauses at night keep sleep from doing its job, so you wake up unrefreshed and foggy.

4/10
Stress can do the same thing. A tense nervous system does not shut off cleanly, and that spillover makes sleep lighter and less restorative.

5/10
Mood matters too. Depression and anxiety can leave people exhausted, even if they spent enough time in bed to satisfy the calendar.

6/10
Alcohol and late caffeine are classic sleep wreckers. One makes sleep more fragmented, the other makes it harder to fall into real rest in the first place.

7/10
If you wake with headaches, dry mouth, or a snoring partner who has become an unwilling sleep detective, pay attention. Those clues matter.

8/10
Sometimes the issue is a medical one like anemia, thyroid problems, diabetes, or medication side effects. The body is annoyingly good at using fatigue as an early warning light.

9/10
If this is happening often, track bedtime, wake time, snoring, alcohol, caffeine, naps, and how you feel on waking. Patterns show up faster on paper than in memory.

10/10
If tiredness is persistent, getting worse, or comes with other symptoms, get checked. “I slept enough” is useful information, but it is not a diagnosis.
