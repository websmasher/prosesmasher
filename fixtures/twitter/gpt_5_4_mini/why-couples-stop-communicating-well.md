1/10 Couples usually do not stop communicating because they run out of words. They stop because talking starts to feel useless, risky, or exhausting.

2/10 Early on, conversation is easy. Later, work, sleep, kids, money, and stress take more of the energy, so people default to logistics and short replies.

3/10 One common pattern is the demand-withdraw loop. One partner pushes for a talk, the other shuts down, and both people end up feeling unheard.

4/10 Another problem is assumptions. A late reply becomes proof of not caring. A short tone becomes proof of contempt.

5/10 Stress makes this worse. It narrows attention, lowers patience, and turns neutral comments into threats.

6/10 Couples also drift apart on goals. If you stop checking what matters to each person, conversation shrinks to scheduling and cleanup.

7/10 The big names matter here too. Criticism, contempt, defensiveness, and stonewalling are not rare disasters. They are common habits that slowly poison the room.

8/10 A lot of fights are not really about dishes, money, or plans. They are about fairness, respect, trust, and feeling like a team.

9/10 What helps is boring and effective. Talk about one issue at a time, use real examples, time the conversation well, and repair fast when you miss.

10/10 If every hard talk ends in shutdown, panic, or contempt, get help. Silence can look calm from far away. Up close, it is usually fear with better manners.
