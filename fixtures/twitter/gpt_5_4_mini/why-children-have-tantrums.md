1/10 Tantrums are not a mystery, and they are not proof your child is "bad." They usually mean a small brain hit a big feeling and ran out of brakes.

2/10 Toddlers want things hard and fast. They also have weak impulse control, short patience, and very little ability to explain what is wrong.

3/10 That is why tantrums show up around hunger, tiredness, transitions, and frustration. The banana broke, the cup changed, and suddenly life is over.

4/10 A tantrum is often a communication failure. Your child may be tired, overwhelmed, sick, or upset, but does not have the words to say it cleanly.

5/10 The home environment matters too. If screaming sometimes wins candy, screen time, or a delay, the tantrum gets a lesson: this works.

6/10 During a tantrum, long explanations usually fail. Use short language, keep the boundary, and focus on safety instead of a perfect speech.

7/10 The goal is not to negotiate with a child who is flooded. The goal is to help the body settle first, then talk once the storm passes.

8/10 Prevention is boring, which is why it works. Sleep, snacks, routines, warnings before transitions, and simple feeling words all reduce the odds.

9/10 Tantrums that are daily, severe, or paired with self-harm, aggression, language delay, or big sleep problems deserve a closer look. Pain and stress can hide underneath the behavior.

10/10 Most tantrums fade as children get better language, better regulation, and better routines. The job is not to eliminate every outburst, it is to lower the fuel and teach a better way through it.
