1/10 Social media does not usually wreck attention in one dramatic hit. It wears focus down through repeated interruption.

2/10 Every notification asks for a slice of your attention. Every slice has a cost, even when it feels tiny.

3/10 The brain pays a switching tax each time you move from one task to a feed and back again. That tax adds up fast.

4/10 Scrolling trains you to expect novelty on demand. Ordinary work starts to feel slow, even when the work has not changed.

5/10 The real problem is not just the app. It is the habit of keeping a feed open while doing something else.

6/10 That split attention makes working memory do extra work. You are holding the task, the post, and the urge to check the next thing.

7/10 Late-night scrolling can make this worse. If sleep gets shorter or lighter, focus usually pays for it the next day.

8/10 Not every platform hits the brain the same way. A group chat, a news feed, and short video all pull attention differently.

9/10 The fix is boring, which is why it works. Turn off alerts, add friction, batch checks, and keep the phone away during real work.

10/10 Social media is powerful because it is relentless, not because it is mysterious. If you want better attention, make interruption harder and one-task time easier.
