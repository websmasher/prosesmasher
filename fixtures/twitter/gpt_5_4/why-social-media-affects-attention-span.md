1/10 Social media affects attention less like a wrecking ball and more like a drip. It trains frequent checking, fast switching, and a constant search for novelty. That can make steady focus feel harder.

2/10 Attention is not one thing. It includes staying with one task, filtering distractions, and holding a goal in mind. Feeds are built to pull all three in the other direction.

3/10 The biggest problem is usually interruption, not some dramatic loss of brain power. If your phone keeps calling you back, your mind keeps paying the restart cost.

4/10 Notifications are a quiet disaster. A badge, a buzz, or a preview can break concentration before you even open the app. That split second adds up fast.

5/10 Feeds also train expectation. When your brain gets used to quick hits of novelty, slower work starts to feel dull after thirty seconds. The work did not get worse. Your tolerance for friction got thinner.

6/10 This gets worse when social media runs in the background. Studying, writing, or working with a feed open means your attention is never fully in one place. You are doing two bad versions of two tasks.

7/10 There is also a sleep problem hiding in plain sight. Night scrolling pushes bedtime later, cuts sleep quality, and makes focus worse the next day. People often blame attention when the bill came from sleep.

8/10 Social media does not hit everyone the same way. People with anxiety, ADHD, low mood, or chronic stress often feel the effect more sharply because attention was already under load.

9/10 The evidence is stronger for short-term distraction and weaker for the claim that social media permanently shrinks attention span across the board. That is less dramatic, but more honest.

10/10 What helps is boring and effective: turn off nonessential notifications, batch your checks, keep the phone out of reach during deep work, and stop taking the app to bed. Attention gets stronger when interruption gets harder.
