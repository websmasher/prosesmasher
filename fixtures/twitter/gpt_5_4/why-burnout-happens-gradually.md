1/10 Burnout rarely starts with a dramatic collapse. It usually starts with small trades that look reasonable: one late night, one skipped break, one more week of running hot.

2/10 That is why people miss it. The early stage often looks like being reliable, responsive, and hard to rattle. From the outside, it can look like high performance.

3/10 Then the body starts keeping score. Sleep gets worse. Patience gets thinner. Work that used to feel normal starts feeling heavy before the day even begins.

4/10 Burnout grows when stress stays high and recovery stays low. Too much workload, too little control, weak support, and constant urgency are a bad mix for a human nervous system.

5/10 Most people expect a single breaking point. The more common pattern is erosion. Energy drops first, then focus, then motivation, then the sense that any of this means much.

6/10 That slow drift matters because you can adapt to unhealthy work. You call it a busy season. Then another busy season. Then your baseline becomes exhaustion with better calendar management.

7/10 Burnout also feeds on mismatch. If the job keeps asking you to move faster, care more, fix more, and absorb more, without enough staff, time, or clarity, strain stops being occasional. It becomes structural.

8/10 This is why surface fixes rarely hold. A day off helps. A meditation app helps. Neither can repair a workload or manager problem that is waiting for you the next morning.

9/10 What helps is less glamorous and more effective: clearer priorities, fewer open loops, real breaks, better staffing, more control over your time, and honest conversations before resentment hardens into detachment.

10/10 If work feels heavier than it should for weeks, do not wait for a meltdown to take it seriously. Burnout is easier to interrupt early than to recover from after your system has been running on fumes for months.
