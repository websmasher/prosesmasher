# Why children have tantrums, and what helps

Tantrums are one of those child behaviors that can make a normal day feel like a hostage negotiation in a grocery aisle. A child is fine one minute, then suddenly collapsed on the floor because the banana broke in half or the blue cup appeared instead of the red one. For adults, the scene looks ridiculous. For the child, the feeling is real.

Most tantrums are not signs that a child is manipulative, broken, or badly parented. They are usually signs that the child’s brain and body are overloaded and they do not yet have the tools to handle the pressure cleanly. That is the core fact. The rest is detail.

## What a tantrum is

A tantrum is a burst of emotional dysregulation. It can look like crying, screaming, kicking, hitting, collapsing, throwing, bolting, or going rigid. In young children, tantrums often come out of frustration, disappointment, hunger, fatigue, or a sudden change in routine.

The early childhood years are a rough time for self-control. Children are gaining language, impulse control, and frustration tolerance at the same time. Those systems do not arrive together. The result is predictable: a child may want something, feel blocked, and have no good way to hold the feeling without spilling it everywhere.

## Why tantrums happen

### Their brains are still under construction

Toddlers and preschoolers have strong feelings and weak brakes. They can want something intensely before they can reason their way through delay, compromise, or disappointment. The parts of the brain that support self-regulation are still developing, so a small problem can feel enormous to them.

That is why tantrums are common around 18 months and through the toddler years. The NHS says tantrums usually start around 18 months and become much less common by age 4. HealthyChildren, the American Academy of Pediatrics site, describes 2-year-olds as children who are testing limits while still lacking much control over emotional impulses.

### They cannot always say what they need

Many tantrums are communication failures. A child may be hungry, tired, overstimulated, bored, or upset, but not able to explain that in a useful way. If a child has limited language, speech delay, or another communication problem, the frustration can show up as a tantrum because the child has no better channel.

This is one reason tantrums can intensify when children are asked to stop one activity and shift to another. The child understands, at a basic level, that something is changing. The child may not understand how to manage that change without a crash.

### They are often triggered by ordinary body states

The usual suspects are boring because they are common: tired, hungry, thirsty, sick, constipated, overtired, or overstimulated. A child who has been at preschool all day, skipped a nap, and missed a snack does not need a moral lecture. They need food, rest, and fewer demands.

Sleep matters a lot. A tired child has less control, less patience, and less capacity to recover from frustration. The same is true when a child is already sick or in pain. Ear pain, reflux, headaches, teething, and constipation can all lower the threshold for an outburst.

### They want independence before they can manage it

Much of toddler behavior is a clash between desire and ability. Children want to choose, do, hold, open, pour, climb, and control the show. They cannot always do those things safely or successfully. The adult blocks the action. The child reacts.

This is why tantrums often appear at the exact moment a parent says no, ends screen time, leaves the park, or insists on shoes. The child is not being dramatic in the abstract. The child is hitting the wall between “I want it” and “I cannot have it.”

### The environment can train the tantrum

Children learn quickly which behaviors change the world around them. If a tantrum sometimes ends with candy, extra screen time, a delayed bedtime, or a parent giving in, the child learns that escalation works. That does not mean the child is scheming. It means the child is a small scientist with excellent data and poor judgment.

Experts often point out the coercive loop: child escalates, parent tries to stop the noise, child escalates more, parent caves. The tantrum gets rewarded, so it returns. This is one reason consistency matters.

### Stress makes everything worse

Stress does not need to be dramatic to matter. A move, a new sibling, conflict at home, daycare problems, family illness, travel, or a disrupted routine can all raise a child’s baseline arousal. HealthyChildren notes that stress can show up in children as crying, screaming, slamming doors, hitting, kicking, or temper tantrums because they do not yet have enough language or control to explain what is going on.

Sometimes tantrums are a child’s stress signal. The behavior is the surface. The stressor is underneath.

## What experts say is normal

Normal does not mean pleasant. It means developmentally expected.

NIMH summarized a preschool study showing that more than 80 percent of preschoolers had at least one tantrum in the prior month, but fewer than 10 percent had them every day. That gap matters. Tantrums happen a lot in early childhood. Daily, severe tantrums are a different category.

The pattern also changes with age. Tantrums are most common in toddlers and early preschoolers, then gradually fade as language, planning, and emotional control improve. By age 4, they are far less common. If a child is getting older and the tantrums are not easing, the problem deserves a closer look.

## What to do during a tantrum

The goal in the moment is not to win. It is to keep everyone safe, avoid feeding the behavior, and help the child get back online.

### Stay calm and use fewer words

Long explanations during a tantrum are usually wasted. The child is not available for a seminar. Use a calm voice, simple language, and very short sentences. If the child is overloaded, more talking is just more noise.

### Set the boundary once

State the limit clearly, then stop negotiating. “We are leaving now.” “No, you may not hit.” “The tablet is done.” Repeating the same rule over and over can turn into a wrestling match. It also teaches the child that persistence may pay off.

### Keep everyone safe

Move dangerous objects away. Separate siblings if needed. Hold the line on hitting, biting, or throwing hard objects. If the tantrum is escalating into danger, the priority is safety, not perfect behavior management.

### Do not reward the tantrum

If the tantrum is about a nonessential request, do not give the child the thing they are screaming for just to stop the noise. That turns the tantrum into a successful strategy. The child will remember.

### Wait for the reset

Many children cannot calm down through logic. They calm down through time, steady presence, and the nervous system finally settling. Once the child is back in a usable state, then you can talk about what happened.

## What helps prevent tantrums

You cannot prevent every meltdown. You can reduce the odds.

### Protect sleep, food, and transitions

Bedtime, snacks, and predictable routines are not glamorous, but they work. A child who is well rested and not running on fumes has a much larger emotional buffer. Warn before transitions. Give a countdown. Offer a choice where you can, but keep the choice small and real.

### Teach words for feelings and needs

Children need scripts before they can use scripts. Teach simple phrases like “I’m mad,” “help me,” “one more minute,” “I’m hungry,” and “I need a break.” If a child can say what is wrong, the tantrum has less work to do.

### Notice patterns

Track when tantrums happen. Is it always around dinner, bedtime, screen time, crowded stores, sibling conflict, or leaving the house? Pattern-spotting beats guesswork. Once you know the trigger, you can change the setup.

### Praise the good exits

Notice when your child handles frustration without exploding. That is the behavior you want more of. Praise the child for using words, taking a breath, waiting, or accepting a boundary. Children repeat what gets attention.

### Be consistent

If “no” sometimes means “yes after 12 minutes of screaming,” tantrums become part of the decision tree. Clear, repeatable responses are less exciting, and more effective.

## When tantrums may signal something bigger

Some tantrums are more than age-appropriate frustration. Call your pediatrician or seek child mental health support if the tantrums are very frequent, very intense, or getting worse rather than better.

Red flags include tantrums that happen daily, last a long time, involve serious aggression, or include self-injury such as head banging, biting, or hitting the head against objects. Seek help if a child cannot calm down at all, seems miserable between tantrums, or the outbursts happen across settings, not just at home.

It also makes sense to ask for help if tantrums come with language delay, social communication concerns, sleep problems, developmental regression, or signs of autism, ADHD, anxiety, depression, trauma, or oppositional behavior. Severe tantrums can be part of those pictures.

Breath-holding, fainting, or collapse during a tantrum also needs medical attention. So do tantrums that seem tied to pain, headaches, stomach pain, constipation, fever, or other physical symptoms.

The NIMH study on preschool tantrums is a useful rule of thumb here: common tantrums are common. Daily or severe tantrums are unusual and worth checking.

## What parents should remember

Tantrums are not proof that a child is bad or a parent is failing. They are a sign that the child has a feeling bigger than the tools they have available at that age. The job is to reduce the fuel, hold the limit, and teach better ways to handle the next wave.

That means less panic, less bargaining, and more consistency. The tantrum will not disappear because you explained your position with courtroom precision. It will improve when the child’s body is regulated, the environment is predictable, and the child slowly learns better ways to ask for what they need.
