Stress is not just a mental state. It is a full-body alarm, and when it stays on too long, your body starts paying the bill.

That can look like tight shoulders, jaw clenching, stomach issues, poor sleep, headaches, a racing heart, feeling wired, or feeling weirdly flat. A lot of people think they are just “bad at coping” when the body is doing exactly what an overloaded body does.

Stress changes how you sleep, how you digest food, how patient you feel, how clearly you think, and how much energy you have left by the end of the day. It can make small tasks feel loud. It can make rest feel impossible. It can leave you tired and tense at the same time, which is a very rude combination.

If you want to slow stress down, start smaller than your brain wants.

Eat something steady.
Go outside for ten minutes.
Move your body without turning it into a self-improvement project.
Put one task on paper instead of carrying twelve in your head.
Lower the noise for an hour.
Go to bed at a time your body can trust.

You do not need a perfect morning routine and a candle that smells like emotional maturity.

You need fewer signals of danger, more repetition, and more recovery than you think.

And if stress is showing up as chest pain, panic, constant insomnia, or a level of exhaustion that does not lift, get checked. Sometimes the body is stressed. Sometimes it is stressed and something else is going on too.
