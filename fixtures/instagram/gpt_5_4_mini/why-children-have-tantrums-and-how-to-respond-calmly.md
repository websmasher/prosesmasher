Tantrums are not a sign that your child is trying to ruin your day on purpose. They usually mean the system is overloaded and the child does not have the skills to hold it together yet.

That overload can come from hunger, fatigue, too much noise, a hard transition, or a big feeling that landed faster than words did. A small problem in adult terms can feel enormous in child terms. The banana broke. The park ended. The answer was no. The emotional volume goes straight to max.

The calmer response is usually simpler than people want it to be. Keep your voice low. Use fewer words. Set the limit once. Stay close if your child needs co-regulation, but do not turn the moment into a negotiation.

You are not trying to win an argument in the middle of a meltdown. You are trying to help a nervous system settle.

That means predictable routines help. So do snacks, sleep, and warnings before transitions. It also means teaching your child a few simple scripts before the next hard moment arrives: “help me,” “I’m mad,” “one more minute,” “I need a break.”

When the storm passes, that is the time for teaching. Not during the screaming. Not during the floor collapse. After.

If tantrums are very frequent, very intense, or paired with aggression, self-harm, speech delays, or big behavior changes, it is worth asking for help. Otherwise, start with the boring stuff that works: calm voice, clear limits, fewer words, and a child who feels held instead of handled.
