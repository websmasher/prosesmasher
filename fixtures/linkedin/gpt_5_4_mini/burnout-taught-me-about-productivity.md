Burnout taught me that productivity is not about doing more.

For a while, I thought I was being productive because I was always available. I answered messages fast. I packed my calendar. I kept a dozen tabs open and told myself that meant I was moving quickly.

It did not.

What I was doing was draining my attention faster than I could recover it. I was busy, but not effective. I was reacting to everything and thinking deeply about very little. The worst part is that burnout does not always show up as collapse. Sometimes it shows up as slower judgment, lower patience, and the strange feeling that every task costs too much.

That changed how I work.

Now I measure productivity by quality of attention, not volume of activity. Can I finish one important thing without constant context switching? Can I make a decision without exhausting myself? Do I have enough space in the day to do work that requires thought, not just speed?

I also stopped treating rest like a reward for overworking. Rest is part of the process. Sleep makes my thinking cleaner. Short breaks keep me from making sloppy decisions. A walk can do more for my output than another hour of pushing through fatigue.

The practical lesson for me was simple:

- Protect your best hours for your hardest work.
- Stop filling every gap just because it is empty.
- Build in recovery before you feel broken.
- Pay attention to what your output looks like on low sleep, high stress, and no margin.

Burnout taught me that sustainable productivity is not heroic. It is repeatable.

I still care about ambition. I just care more now about whether my system lets me do good work tomorrow, not just get through today.
