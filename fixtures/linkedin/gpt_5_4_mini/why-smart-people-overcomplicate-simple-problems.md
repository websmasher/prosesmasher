Smart people do not overcomplicate simple problems because they love complexity.

They do it because simple answers can feel too exposed.

If the problem is small, clean, and obvious, there is nowhere to hide. There is no fog of jargon, no elegant framework, no dramatic analysis to perform. Just a plain decision, and the possibility of being judged for making it look too easy.

That is uncomfortable for people who have built an identity around being careful, sharp, and right.

So they keep working.
They add one more layer.
One more model.
One more meeting.
One more round of thinking that feels responsible and productive, but is really just a delay with a nice shirt on.

I have watched smart people turn a 15-minute decision into a weeklong project because clarity felt too final. Once you name the real issue, you also name the real tradeoff. Once you choose a simple path, you lose the comfort of pretending the answer is still hidden.

Complexity can also be a kind of protection.

If the problem stays complicated, then the outcome stays uncertain, and uncertainty gives the ego room to breathe. If the answer is obvious and the result is poor, there is nowhere to place the blame. That is a harder thing to sit with than it should be.

The best people I know are not the ones who can make everything sound sophisticated. They are the ones who can strip a mess down to its core without needing applause for it.

They can say, “This is the real issue.”
They can name the next move.
They can leave their intelligence out of the room long enough to let it be useful.

That is the real test. Not how many layers you can add.

How quickly you can tell the truth about what is already simple.
