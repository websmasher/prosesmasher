Stress at work does not just make people feel worse.

It makes their decisions worse.

You can watch it happen in real time. A team under pressure stops comparing options and starts hunting for relief. The quickest answer begins to look like the smartest one. The loudest voice starts to sound decisive. A shaky deadline gets treated like a law of physics.

That is when people accept bad timelines because pushing back feels awkward. They delay feedback because the conversation feels heavy. They keep weak plans alive because changing course would mean admitting the first plan was weak.

Stress shrinks judgment. People rely more on habit, hierarchy, and certainty. They pay less attention to second-order effects. They confuse motion with progress because motion feels better than sitting with ambiguity for five uncomfortable minutes.

You do not fix that with a speech about resilience.

You fix it with operating habits.

Before a costly decision, force a short pause. Ten minutes is often enough to stop urgency from impersonating wisdom.

Write down the decision, the main risk, and what would make you change your mind. If the reasoning looks thin on paper, it will look worse once the decision is live.

Give someone explicit permission to challenge the plan. Stress makes groups obedient, and obedient groups miss obvious problems.

Then ask one simple question: what breaks if we wait a day, and what breaks if we rush now?

Stress will always show up at work. The goal is not perfect calm. The goal is a team that notices when pressure is shrinking its thinking, then slows down long enough to choose instead of react.
