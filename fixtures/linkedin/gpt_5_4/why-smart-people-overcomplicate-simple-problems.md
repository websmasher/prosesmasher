Smart people do not overcomplicate simple problems because they are confused.

They do it because complexity feels like respect.

If you are used to being rewarded for insight, range, and pattern recognition, a simple answer can feel suspicious. Too plain. Too obvious. Too close to something anyone in the room could have said. So the brain starts decorating. More frameworks. More caveats. More slides. More language between the problem and the decision.

I have done this more than once.

You look at a basic operational issue, ownership is unclear, priorities keep shifting, nobody wants to make the tradeoff, and somehow the room ends up discussing second-order theory like it is a graduate seminar. It feels smart. It also burns a week.

A lot of overcomplication is emotional, not intellectual.

Simple problems are uncomfortable because they usually demand something clean and exposed. Pick one owner. Cut one feature. Say no to one stakeholder. Admit the deadline is fantasy. None of that is hard to understand. It is hard to say out loud.

Complexity can be a very polished form of avoidance.

The people I trust most at work are rarely the ones with the longest explanation. They are the ones who can reduce the mess without pretending the mess is not there. They can say, "This is the real constraint. This is the decision. This is what we do next."

That takes more confidence than sounding impressive.

If a problem keeps expanding every time you discuss it, ask a rude but useful question:

What would this look like if we were forced to make it boring?

Boring is underrated.

Boring ships.

Boring clarifies ownership.

Boring leaves less room for performance and more room for progress.

Sometimes the smartest move in the room is to stop proving you are smart and start making the problem smaller.
