The biggest communication mistake teams make is confusing discussion with decision.

You see it after almost every messy meeting. People talk for 45 minutes, everyone sounds engaged, the call ends, and somehow nobody knows what was decided, who owns what, or when anything is due.

Then the cleanup starts.

One person rewrites the plan in Slack. Another sends a "just to confirm" message. Two people start working on the same thing. Someone waits because they think the decision is still pending. By Thursday, the team is frustrated and nobody can point to the exact moment the work went sideways.

It usually goes sideways in the last two minutes of the meeting.

Teams spend all their energy on the conversation and almost none on the close. That is where the confusion slips in. People hear the same words and leave with different interpretations. "We should move fast" sounds clear until one person hears "ship today" and another hears "prepare a draft for next week."

The fix is plain, and it saves a ridiculous amount of time.

Before any meeting ends, ask four questions out loud:

What did we decide?

Who owns the next step?

What is the deadline?

What is still unresolved?

If the room cannot answer those four questions in one minute, the meeting produced conversation, not clarity.

Good teams do not assume shared understanding. They verify it. They name owners. They use dates instead of "soon." They write the decision down while everyone is still there to correct it.

**Clear communication is not about sounding polished. It is about leaving less room for guesswork.**

If your team keeps repeating the same mistakes, check the handoff between talk and action. That is where a lot of work gets lost.
